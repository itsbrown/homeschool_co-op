Section 2: Week One - Introduction to Orthography
From Seekers Fall Level 3 Literacy Essentials.pdf, Pages 1-2. Focus on assessment for handwriting/manuscript/cursive, phonogram introduction (42-26-72 hierarchy), and multisensory practice (air-writing, dictation).
DAY 1 - ASSESSMENT
Determine what, if any, handwriting you will teach. It is my recommendation that you dictate a short paragraph to your students as an assessment tool. Choose something challenging, yet appropriate for their grade level. You might choose something from the literature they will be starting, or part of the Gettysburg Address, Preamble to the Constitution, etc. Dictate a sentence at a time. When dictating sentences you will say the sentence and then the students will repeat the sentence. If it is a particularly long sentence you may repeat it twice and have them repeat it twice. After repeating the sentence they will write it. Then, proceed to the next sentence. (This type of sentence dictation provides you a window into your students' ability to hold a number of words in working memory. It may help you identify students who may be challenged by this skill.) The goals of the dictation will be:
1. To assess the student's handwriting.
2. To in general assess the student's current spelling ability.
3. In addition, you will also be able to determine their ability to organize a paragraph (indent first sentence, start sentences with capitals, use correct punctuation, etc.).
In the past when we have done this, we forewarned the students that they would be choosing their fate. If they showed excellent handwriting, then we would not spend much time on handwriting. If the handwriting was horrible, then they would be demonstrating their need for instruction and practice. You may give them the option to choose manuscript or cursive. Many may never have had cursive instruction; these students should absolutely use manuscript. Another option is to have the entire class use manuscript for the first paragraph and then dictate another paragraph later in the day asking them to write in cursive. There is flexibility here, but the information you are seeking is crucial to future planning.
The beginning of the year is the time to establish your expectations for handwriting. Based on the information gathered from the paragraph writing, decide how to proceed with teaching or remediating handwriting. Many students have had very poor, if any, handwriting instruction. Students who are struggling readers especially need an explicit, consistent manner of letter formation for each of the letters. Consistent letter formation sets the sound/symbol relationship in cement in their brain, rather than on shifting sand. Even if the class has solid manuscript, cursive instruction uses the language of instruction from manuscript. Therefore, we recommend you quickly review manuscript to teach what is lacking while students are learning the phonograms during the first three weeks. The students will be writing the phonograms as part of daily practice, so it is the perfect time to work on consistent, beautiful letter formation. The dialogue for teaching the phonograms and talking through the check points for letter formation are on the back of phonogram cards 1-26.
During week four, if your students are ready, you can begin teaching cursive. The multi-letter phonograms contain many of the common letter connections used in words and so, it is a great way to practice the common cursive connections. If your school has a policy that all essays in the elementary grades must be turned in handwritten, and students are not fluent in cursive, it will be necessary to instruct on cursive at some point early in the school year.
Note: Literacy Essentials handwriting instruction for manuscript is found in the Training Manual, Unit 1 with additional handwriting masters for use in teaching found in the Level Three Copy Masters, Handwriting, Manuscript Handwriting Master D-1. Cursive handwriting instruction is found in the Copy Masters, Handwriting, Cursive Handwriting Master E-3. There are three wall charts preprinted that can be used when teaching handwriting and there are charts you can make recommended for use with handwriting instruction. See completed images in the Copy Masters. Cursive Lowercase by Stroke (Master F-2) and Cursive Uppercase by Stroke (Master F-3).

Stroke Formation: Begin explicit instruction for remediating or teaching manuscript handwriting. Handwriting instruction begins with learning five initial strokes before actual letter formation. Use the Clock Chart which provides a place to demonstrate and organize the five strokes. This first session should accomplish teaching the five strokes: clock stroke; short line stroke; dash line stroke; tall line stroke; slant line stroke. Handwriting instruction and practice will continue as long as necessary until fluency and automaticity are developed.
Session 2 Handwriting: Clock Stroke Letters, Phonograms 1-7
Teach the sound/symbol relationship for the 26 letters of the alphabet using the phonogram cards (1-26) continuing instruction based on your assessment using the dictated paragraph given on Day 1. (The students will write the phonograms as they are learned. So, if performance on the handwriting assessment was excellent you will simply be reviewing manuscript handwriting, merely using it as one of the multisensory routes to mastery of the phonograms.) Discussion of how to teach and practice phonograms is found in the Training Manual, Unit 1, pages 8-12. As well, the instructions can be found within the box of phonogram cards, Teacher Dialogue for Phonogram Instruction. ii - iv, My Turn, Our Turn, Your Turn.
Phonograms 1-26 can be covered in one day using this session and two additional sessions. The majority of the students should know the most common sound (first sound of the phonogram) for the letters of the alphabet. For the first 26 letters/phonograms, most of the explicit teaching and practice will revolve around the multiple sounds of the vowels, [a], [e], [i], [y], [o], [u], and the multiple sounds of [c], [g], and [s]. You will continue to lead practice of those few phonograms along with other new phonograms added over the next several weeks.

Section 3: Marking System for Spelling Analysis
From pasted-text.txt. Use multisensorily (e.g., air-writing while marking) to reinforce phonograms/rules. Tie to trivium: grammar (memorize), logic (debate applications), rhetoric (compose patriotic narratives).
The marking system is a visual, rule-based tool for spelling analysis, integrated into decoding. Markings include:
* Underline Multi-Letter Phonograms: Underline all multi-letter phonograms to show that the multiple letters work together to represent one sound. (e.g., weight, chop, flight)
* Number Non-First Sounds: First sounds of phonograms are understood and not marked. When a phonogram does not represent its first sound, put a number above the phonogram to show which sound is used. (e.g., great², to³, thought³, school²) NOTE: A single vowel saying its name (second sound) is underlined (#3 below), and C and G when followed by e, i, or y say the second sound but do not have a 2 above them.
* Underline Single Vowels Saying Their Name: Underline a single vowel that says its name (its second sound). (e.g., crazy, no, food, be, by) The letter U has two sounds that are considered its "name". When U says /U/, it is underlined (#3) (e.g., cute, funeral). When U says /oo/ (its 3rd sound, 'nickname'), whether due to Silent Final E or an open syllable, the U is underlined (#3) and has a 3 above it (e.g., student³, ruin³, true³).
* Dot Syllable Breaks: Mark syllable breaks with a dot placed within a single space. (e.g., magnetic, table) Identifying syllable breaks is critical for explaining vowel sounds.
* Silent Final E Jobs (1-6):
   * Job 1: The E makes the vowel say its name. Single underline the vowel and consonant; double underline the Silent E and put a 1 after it. (e.g., cake¹, these¹, note¹, cute¹, rule¹)
   * Job 2: English words do not end with V or U, so add Silent Final E. Single underline the v or u; double underline the Silent E and put a 2 after it. (e.g., have², value², blue²)
   * Job 3: The E makes C say /s/ and G say /j/. Single underline the C or G; double underline the Silent E and put a 3 after it. (e.g., dance³, large³)
   * Job 4: English syllables must have a written vowel, so add Silent Final E. Double underline the Silent E and put a 4 after it. (e.g., table⁴, cycle⁴, little⁴, giggle⁴)
   * Job 5: When a base word ends with a single S, add Silent Final E to show it's not a suffix. Single underline the S; double underline the Silent E and put a 5 after it. (e.g., horse⁵, house⁵, lose⁵, raise⁵)
   * Job 6 (Odd Job E): For lengthening words (e.g., awe⁶, ewe⁶), homophones (e.g., or⁶, ore⁶), formerly pronounced E (e.g., one⁶, come⁶), or other reasons (e.g., were⁶, there⁶, where⁶, gone⁶). Double underline the Silent E and put a 6 after it. See The ABC's and All Their Tricks by Margaret Bishop, pp. 105-106.
* Double Underline Silent Letters: Double underline all silent letters (e.g., climb, island, people, talk). The silent phonogram is spoken when saying the "two-way" word for spelling (except Silent Final E). (e.g., climb: say "klimb"; island: say "is land"; talk: say "tawk")
* Caret or Two-Way Cap for Unusual Sounds: Put a ^ (caret) over a phonogram that does not say any of its usual sounds. When spelling, it reminds us to say the phonogram two ways—one in speech rhythm, another for spelling. (e.g., think (thi^nk): say /th//i//ng//k/ but spell /th//i//n//k/; been (bêên): underline double E with ^ and say /b//e//n/ but spell /b//E//n/; said (sâid): underline AI with ^ and say /s//e//d/ but spell /s//A//d/) NOTE: Two-way single vowels are sounded according to syllable type—first sound for closed syllables, second for open syllables, SFE Job 1, or I/O followed by two consonants. (e.g., was: say /w//u//z/ but spell /w//a//z/; the: say /th//u/ but spell /TH//E/)
* Regional Pronunciations: Discuss and adapt markings for variations, using "We say the word one way when we speak but we say it a second way to spell."

Section 5: 44 English Spelling Rules
From Extracted 44 English Spelling Rules.pdf. Teach sequentially, debating applications (e.g., Rule 11 for /j/ in "large³" symbolizing soft justice in self-governance).
1. Consistent vowels (A, E, O, U): These vowels usually say their long sounds (/A/, /E/, /O/, /U/) at the end of a syllable (e.g., pa-per, me-ter).
2. Less consistent vowels (I and Y): They often say /E/ or /I/ at the end of a syllable (e.g., happy, tyrant).
3. English words do not end with I: Y stands in for I at the end (e.g., lady, deny).
4. Vowel flexibility: I and O may say their long sounds when followed by two consonants (e.g., find, cold).
5. AY spelling: Usually spells the long A sound at the end of a base word (e.g., day, away).
6. OR with W: OR says /er/ when preceded by W (e.g., work, worm).
7. AR with W: AR says /or/ when preceded by W (e.g., war, warn).
8. Unstressed AR and OR: These usually say /er/ in unstressed syllables (e.g., dollar, doctor).
9. Silent Final E Job 1: Makes the vowel say its name (e.g., cake, time).
10. Silent Final E Job 2: Prevents ending in V or U (e.g., have, value).
11. Silent Final E Job 3: Makes C say /s/ and G say /j/ (e.g., dance, large).
12. Silent Final E Job 4: Adds a vowel to syllables lacking one (e.g., table, giggle).
13. Silent Final E Job 5: Distinguishes base words ending with S from suffixes (e.g., horse, house).
14. Odd Job Silent E: Used for other reasons like lengthening or homophones (e.g., awe, ewe).
15. IE vs. EI: IE usually spells /E/ except after C or when pronounced /A/ (e.g., piece, ceiling).
16. Q followed by U: Always true in English spelling (e.g., queen).
17. C before E, I, or Y: Usually says /s/ (e.g., city, ceiling).
18. G before E, I, or Y: May say /j/ (e.g., giant, gym).
19. Floss Rule: Double F, L, S after a single vowel in short words (e.g., off, bell).
20. CK, DGE, TCH rules: Used after a single vowel sound (e.g., back, badge, catch).
21. The letter Z: Spells /z/ at the beginning of base words (e.g., zoo).
22. The letter X: Never followed by S (e.g., foxes, excite).
23. Double consonants: Often close a syllable (e.g., happy, rabbit).
24. SH usage: Found at the beginning or end of syllables but not after the first unless in -ship (e.g., shut, friendship).
25. TI, CI, SI spell /sh/: Used at the beginning of later syllables (e.g., nation, special).
26. Suffixes with S: S and ES form plurals and verb endings (e.g., cats, wishes).
27. F to V change: Plural suffixes change F or FE to V (e.g., wolves, knives).
28. Dropping Silent E: Drop E before adding a vowel suffix unless it’s needed for clarity (e.g., hope → hoping, change → changing).
29. 1-1-1 Rule: Double the final consonant before adding a vowel suffix if the word ends in one vowel and one consonant (e.g., hop → hopping).
30. 2-1-1 Accent Rule: Double the final consonant in multi-syllable words if the stress is on the last syllable (e.g., admit → admitted).
31. Y to I Rule: Change final Y to I when adding a suffix unless the suffix begins with I (e.g., cry → cried, cry → crying).
32. ED endings: Pronounced /d/, /t/, or /ed/ depending on the final consonant sound (e.g., jumped, started).
33. ALL and FULL: Drop one L when forming longer words (e.g., almost, careful).
34. Prefixes doubling consonants: When the root starts with the same letter as the prefix ends (e.g., unnecessary, misspell).
35. Syllables and vowels: Each syllable has one vowel sound.
36. Closed syllables: End with a consonant, and the vowel says its short sound (e.g., cat).
37. Open syllables: End with a vowel, and the vowel says its long sound (e.g., me).
38. R-controlled vowels: Combine with R to form new sounds (e.g., car, bird).
39. Compound words: Divide at the boundary (e.g., backpack).
40. Capitalize I and sentences: Pronouns, sentence starts, and titles.
41. Capitalize proper nouns: Names of people, places, and specific entities.
42. Contractions: Apostrophes replace omitted letters (e.g., can’t, won’t).
43. Possession with apostrophes: Add ’s for singular or s’ for plural nouns (e.g., Mary’s, boys’).
44. Decoding strategy: Break down multi-syllable words for clarity and pronunciation.
These rules form a foundational system for understanding English spelling and reading.
